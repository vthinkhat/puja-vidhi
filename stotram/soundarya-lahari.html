Shloka 15: Sarva Shukla Saraswati

Context

This is the fifteenth shloka of the Soundarya Lahari, describing one of the three primary ways in which the blessings (kāṭākṣa) of Goddess Saraswati can be received. In this form, known as Sarva Shukla Saraswati (completely adorned in white), She bestows Vāk-dāthi—the divine gift of eloquent and refined speech.

Description of the Goddess

Sarva Shukla Saraswati – the all-white form of the Goddess
  • Completely white in color.
  • She shines with the radiance of the autumn (Sharad) moon.
  • A crescent moon adorns her beautifully braided hair.
  • She holds four attributes in her hands:
    • Varada Mudra (gesture of granting boons)
    • Abhaya Mudra (gesture of protection)
    • A crystal (sphatika) rosary
    • A sacred book

Result of Worship

Those who meditate upon this form are blessed with a continuous stream of sweet and powerful speech—likened to honey, milk, and grapes.

Symbolism

  • Honey: Speech that delights and brings happiness to others.
  • Milk: Nourishing words that enrich both speaker and listener.
  • Grapes: Effortless, sweet, and immediately comprehensible expression.

Why "Grapes"?

In Sanskrit poetics, styles of expression are classified by pāka (processing metaphor):

  • Narikela-pāka (coconut): Dense and difficult (e.g., Amuktamalyada)
  • Kadalee-pāka (banana): Softer, easier to digest
  • Drākṣā-pāka (grape): No processing needed—instantly sweet and enjoyable

Grape-like speech is thus seen as the highest form—effortless and universally appreciated, a true mark of Saraswati’s grace.

Mantra Aspect

The bīja mantra “Śreem” is embedded within the shloka, invoking the Goddess’s energy of abundance and sweetness. Reciting it invokes Saraswati’s blessing on all levels of communication.

Practical Note

This form is especially helpful for students, speakers, and professionals. Teaching children to chant and meditate on this shloka helps cultivate grace, clarity, and beauty in their communication.

Shloka 16: Aruṇa Saraswati

Context

This is the sixteenth shloka of the Soundarya Lahari, and the second in the Sarasvata Prayoga series. Here, the blessings of Saraswati are received through śrujana-ātmataḥ—a deep, original, and spontaneous creativity.

Description of the Goddess

Aruṇa Saraswati – the dawn-hued form of the Goddess
  • This form is known as Aruṇa Saraswati, associated with the reddish hue of dawn.
  • Though the color “red” is not directly mentioned in the verse, the term Aruṇa (the rosy glow of sunrise) suffuses the imagery.
  • She is visualized as the gentle awakening light that coaxes the heart’s lotus into bloom.

Result of Worship

Those who meditate on this form receive the boon of authentic creativity—a constant stream of refined and original ideas, rising from within rather than through imitation.

Symbolism

  • The rays of Aruṇa represent the compassionate energy of the Goddess, awakening dormant emotions and creative potential.
  • Suppressed ideas and forgotten feelings blossom under Her influence, enabling eloquent and meaningful expression.

Mantra Aspect

Special emphasis is placed on the "Īm" sound in the word bhavatheem—a potent bīja mantra linked to Saraswati.

Chanting with focus on “Īm” helps unlock inner creativity and intellectual clarity.

Traditional Story: A tale speaks of a dull-witted boy who accidentally heard the sound “Īm” from an injured wild boar. Repeating the sound unintentionally, he attracted Saraswati’s grace and blossomed into a great scholar. This illustrates the transformative power of the “Īm” bīja, even when used unknowingly.

Practical Note

Encourage children to regularly chant this shloka (pārāyaṇa). The repeated resonance of “Īm” supports both creative development and cognitive growth in a natural, spiritually aligned manner.

Shloka 17: Mātrikā Vākdevatās

Context

This is the seventeenth shloka of the Soundarya Lahari, and the third in the Sarasvata Prayoga series. Here, Saraswati’s blessing manifests as Sārthakatā—true fulfillment and meaningful application of knowledge and speech.

Result of Worship

This shloka ensures that one’s learning, creativity, and eloquence serve a meaningful purpose—benefiting both the individual and the world. Without such divine direction, even great talents may remain unfulfilled or misused.

Symbolism

The Eight Vāk Devatās (Goddesses of Speech) encircling the Divine Mother
  • In the 7th Āvaraṇa of the Śrī Vidyā Kadgamālā stotram, eight Vāk Devīs (goddesses of speech) are invoked:
    • Vāsinī
    • Kāmeśvarī
    • Modhinī
    • Vimalā
    • Aruṇā
    • Jayinī
    • Sarveśvarī
    • Kaulinī
  • These goddesses are described as surrounding the Divine Mother, shining like eight-faceted moonstones.
  • Meditating on Saraswati along with these eight powers results in extraordinary fluency, beauty, and power of expression.

Inner Symbolism

These eight Vāk Devīs are not merely symbolic; they govern different stages of speech within us: from ideation to articulation. The Sanskrit Akṣaramālā (alphabet) is also divided into eight groups, each ruled by one of these Devīs.

Practical Note

Chanting this shloka correctly grants:

  • Rich vocabulary
  • Clarity in pronunciation
  • Eloquence and refined speech

No formal initiation is necessary. However, accurate pronunciation, proper intonation, and correct stress on syllables are essential.

Warning: Incorrect recitation can lead to unintended effects. Ensure children and students are properly taught to chant this shloka with precision and understanding.
